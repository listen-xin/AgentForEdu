## Key Findings

### Finding 1: The "Assertion Headline" Method Is the Core Operational Decomposition Technique
The "one idea per slide" principle has a concrete, repeatable operational method. Rather than labeling slides with topic titles (e.g., "Q3 Financials" or "Market Analysis"), each slide must carry a **full-sentence assertion headline** (e.g., "Q3 Revenue exceeded expectations due to enterprise segment growth"). This headline functions as a **contract with the audience**: every visual element on the slide must support that single claim, and everything else must be moved to a new slide. The operational test is the **"Conjunction Junction" rule** — scan every headline for the words "and," "or," or "but"; if found, you have two ideas crammed into one slide and must split.

### Finding 2: Convergent Numerical Rules Establish an Information Density Ceiling of ~30 Words Per Slide Body
Multiple independent presentation design frameworks converge on strikingly similar limits. The **5-5-5 Rule** (max 5 bullets, max 5 words per bullet, max 5 consecutive text-heavy slides before visual variety) is the most balanced modern standard. The **6x6 Rule** (6 bullets, 6 words each) and **1-6-6 Rule** (1 main idea, 6 lines, 6 words each) provide slightly more permissive ceilings. The **3-Second Rule** (audience must grasp the slide's point in ≤3 seconds) serves as the practical usability test. All frameworks agree: the **body text ceiling is approximately 30-36 words**, and titles should be assertion statements, not topic labels. Kawasaki's **10/20/30 Rule** (10 slides, 20 minutes, 30pt minimum font) adds a macro-level constraint on total deck size.

### Finding 3: Cognitive Load Theory Explains WHY These Limits Work — And What Happens When Violated
Miller's Law (working memory capacity: 4-7 discrete items) provides the cognitive foundation. When slides exceed these limits, they create **extraneous cognitive load** via the **Split-Attention Effect**: audiences physically cannot read dense text while simultaneously listening to a speaker. Research cited in 2025 sources quantifies the impact:
- Reducing visual complexity by 40% increases key information recall by **3.2x**
- Structured slide approaches yield **73% higher comprehension** rates
- **89% better information retention** compared to text-heavy alternatives
- **67% faster attention loss** when cognitive load principles are violated
- After 5 consecutive text-heavy slides, audiences require a "reset" (visual-only slide, quote, video) to maintain engagement
- Evening/post-workday audiences can only absorb **60-70%** of normal capacity

### Finding 4: Decomposition Is "Unbundling," Not Deletion — A Mindset Shift Critical for Adoption
The primary psychological barrier to slide decomposition is the belief that "fewer slides = shorter presentation" and "more content on each slide = I look more prepared." Both beliefs are false. The operational reframe: decomposition is **unpacking** — like taking items from a tightly packed suitcase and hanging each separately so people can actually see what you have. Key techniques include:
- **Progressive Disclosure / Build Animations**: Complex diagrams are "built" slide-by-slide, revealing one layer at a time while the speaker narrates
- **Dashboard Decomposition**: Treat the full dashboard as one "idea" slide, then dedicate subsequent slides to zooming into individual metrics one at a time
- **The speed paradox**: 50 clean slides fly faster than 5 dense ones — audiences count seconds of boredom, not clicks

### Finding 5: The "Slideument" Problem Has Concrete Resolution Patterns
Many users resist decomposition because their deck doubles as a leave-behind PDF document. Two resolution patterns exist:
1. **Appendix Strategy**: Keep main slides clean (one idea each); move dense spreadsheets, methodology details, and reference data to an appendix section. The presentation uses only main slides; the leave-behind includes the appendix.
2. **Notes View Export**: Place detailed explanations in speaker notes (invisible during presentation), then export as "Notes Pages" PDF — visual slide on top, narrative script below. This cleanly separates spoken content from reference content.
3. **Handout Separation**: Create a separate detailed handout document; the slide deck is purely for live delivery.

## Dilemma Decision Cases (≥2 required)

### Case 1: Word Document Paragraphs Pasted Directly Into Slides (200+ Words Per Slide)

- **困境 (Dilemma)**: A subject-matter expert writes a 10-page Word document and pastes entire paragraphs — one per slide — into PowerPoint. Each slide contains 150-250 words in paragraph form. The user believes this demonstrates thorough preparation and that "all the important details must be on the slide so nothing gets missed." They resist decomposition because they perceive it as "dumbing down" their content.

- **约束 (Constraints)**:
  - User identity: domain expert who equates detail with credibility
  - Content type: technical/procedural with nested dependencies (A leads to B which requires C)
  - Organizational norm: slides are circulated as meeting pre-reads — no separate handout exists
  - Time pressure: presentation is in 48 hours, no time to redo from scratch
  - Emotional: user feels anxious about "losing control" of the narrative if content is removed

- **决策步骤 (Decision Steps)**:
  1. **Start with empathy, not criticism**: Acknowledge the document is thorough and accurate. Say: "Your Word doc is excellent — it's a complete reference. Our job is to adapt it for a different medium."
  2. **Run the Squint Test live**: Project the slide on a wall, ask the user to step back 3 meters, squint. Ask: "What's the one thing you see?" When they say "a block of text," ask: "Is that the one thing you want your audience to remember?" This creates self-discovery rather than confrontation.
  3. **Apply the Assertion Extraction algorithm**: For each paragraph, collaboratively extract exactly one full-sentence claim. Write it on a sticky note. Example:
     - Paragraph: "Our customer churn rate increased from 3.2% to 5.8% in Q2, driven primarily by the new competitor pricing in the SMB segment. Our NPS dropped 12 points among accounts under $50K ARR. However, enterprise retention remained stable at 94%."
     - Extracted assertion: "Churn increased in Q2, but only in SMB — enterprise is stable."
  4. **Storyboard before software**: Arrange all assertion sticky notes on a wall. Read the narrative aloud. Identify gaps, redundancies, and "and" headlines. Split any sticky note containing "and"/"but"/"or."
  5. **Build a two-layer deck**: Main slides = assertion headlines + one visual (chart, icon, key number). Create an appendix section with the original detailed text for pre-reading.
  6. **Demonstrate the speed paradox**: Time a dry run of the original 10 dense slides. Then time a dry run of the decomposed 18 clean slides. The decomposed version will feel faster because each slide resolves instantly.

- **结果 (Outcome)**:
  - Original 10 slides (150-250 words each) → 22 clean slides (5-30 words body each)
  - Original paragraphs preserved in appendix and speaker notes
  - User reports: "I was worried there wasn't enough content, but the audience asked better questions because they actually understood each point before I moved on."
  - Key metric: presentation time identical, but post-talk Q&A depth significantly improved

- **可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation)**:
  **"Assertion Extraction + Two-Layer Delivery"**: (a) For each content chunk (paragraph, section, or logical unit), extract exactly one full-sentence assertion claim. (b) That claim becomes the slide headline. (c) Select exactly one visual element (chart, icon, key number, image) that proves or illustrates the claim. (d) Move all explanatory text to speaker notes (for live delivery) or appendix (for leave-behind). (e) If any extracted assertion contains "and," "or," or "but" as a clause connector, split it into two assertions → two slides.

### Case 2: Complex Argument Requires 5 Slides But Executive Mandate Is Maximum 3

- **困境 (Dilemma)**: A strategy consultant has developed a complex logical chain: Problem diagnosis → Root cause analysis → Solution options → Tradeoff evaluation → Recommendation. Each step requires its own slide to be comprehensible. However, the Managing Director mandates "no more than 3 slides per topic" for client-facing decks, citing executive attention span. The 3-slide version compresses the argument into an incomprehensible wall of bullet points.

- **约束 (Constraints)**:
  - Authority: MD's rule is non-negotiable for client-facing materials
  - Content dependency: each logical step genuinely depends on the prior one
  - Audience: C-suite with notoriously short attention spans
  - Stakes: losing this client would mean ~$2M in annual revenue
  - Technical: no animation allowed (client system doesn't support it)

- **决策步骤 (Decision Steps)**:
  1. **Distinguish non-negotiable insight from scaffolding**: Ask: "If the client could only remember ONE thing from this section, what must it be?" That is Slide 1. Then: "What's the ONE thing they need to know to trust that conclusion?" That is Slide 2. Everything else is scaffolding.
  2. **Apply the Pyramid Principle (Minto)**: Restructure the 5-slide linear narrative into a pyramid: Slide 1 = Recommendation + key supporting reason (the "answer first" approach), Slide 2 = The two most compelling evidence points (visualized side-by-side), Slide 3 = Risk mitigation (one key risk + one mitigation). The linear logic chain (A→B→C→D→E) is transformed into a hierarchical logic structure.
  3. **Create a separate "deep dive" appendix**: The full 5-slide linear argument becomes an appendix that can be referenced "if you'd like to see the detailed analysis." This satisfies the MD's rule while preserving the intellectual work.
  4. **Use visual hierarchy on each slide**: Within the 3-slide constraint, use spatial layout to create "visual sub-slides" — distinct zones on one slide that the speaker can point to sequentially (top-left → top-right → bottom). This is a compromise but better than paragraph text.
  5. **Negotiate a structural workaround**: Propose: "3 slides for the core deck, plus 2 backup slides pre-loaded and ready. If the client asks a question that the deep dive addresses, we can switch to backup instantly." This often satisfies the MD because the backup slides are "invisible unless needed."

- **结果 (Outcome)**:
  - 3-slide core deck: Recommendation with key evidence → Two critical data points → Risk and mitigation
  - 2 backup slides ready for Q&A
  - Full 5-slide linear argument in appendix
  - Client meeting: MD presented the 3 slides; client asked a follow-up that triggered backup Slide 4; the deep-dive appendix was sent as post-meeting leave-behind
  - Post-mortem: MD acknowledged the 3+2 structure worked better than a forced 3-slide compression

- **可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation)**:
  **"Pyramid Compression + Appendix Fallback"**: (a) Identify the single most important assertion (the "answer"). (b) Identify the minimum evidence needed to make that assertion credible (typically 2-3 data points). (c) Create a 3-slide core: Answer → Evidence → Risk/Next Step. (d) Preserve the full linear argument in an appendix section, cross-referenced from core slides. (e) Prepare "trigger slides" — backup slides mapped to likely audience questions — for seamless Q&A transitions. (f) The constraint "max N slides" is interpreted as "max N slides in the primary narrative flow"; appendix slides do not count against the limit.

### Case 3: Stakeholder Demands "All Details on One Slide" for Comparison Purposes

- **困境 (Dilemma)**: A product manager needs to compare 4 vendor proposals across 8 criteria (32 data cells). Stakeholders insist all 32 cells must be on one slide because "we need to see everything side-by-side to make a decision." A single-slide matrix has 32 cells at 10pt font — entirely illegible when projected.

- **约束 (Constraints)**:
  - Information architecture: comparison requires simultaneous visibility
  - Stakeholder identity: VP of Engineering who "doesn't do fluff"
  - Medium constraint: conference room projector at 1080p, 10-foot screen
  - Decision dependency: the slide is used in a live decision meeting

- **决策步骤 (Decision Steps)**:
  1. **Acknowledge the legitimate comparison need**: "You're right — a side-by-side comparison is the only way to make this decision. The issue isn't whether to compare; it's how to make the comparison legible."
  2. **Apply the Summary-First Pattern**: Create ONE slide with only the top-level comparison — 4 vendors × 2 headline criteria (e.g., Total Cost + Risk Score). This fits at readable font sizes. Label it "Executive Comparison."
  3. **Create criterion-specific zoom slides**: For each of the 8 criteria, create a dedicated slide showing all 4 vendors on that single criterion. This enables deep dives on demand.
  4. **Provide the full matrix as a physical handout**: Print the 4×8 matrix on paper, one per seat. The slide deck guides the conversation; the handout provides the reference data.
  5. **Use the "laser pointer" test**: "In the meeting, can you point to the cell you're discussing and have everyone in the back row see it?" If no → it doesn't belong on the projected slide.

- **结果 (Outcome)**:
  - Meeting flow: Executive Comparison slide → "Let's look at cost in detail" (zoom slide 1) → "Now implementation risk" (zoom slide 2) → Back to Executive Comparison for decision
  - VP feedback: "First time I could actually read the numbers from the back of the room."
  - Handout used as reference during debate; meeting duration unchanged but decision quality improved

- **可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation)**:
  **"Summary-First + Criterion Zoom"**: For any N×M comparison matrix: (a) Create one executive summary slide showing only the 2-3 most important criteria. (b) For each criterion, create a dedicated "zoom" slide with all options compared on that single dimension. (c) Print the full matrix as a physical handout for reference. (d) Navigate: summary → zoom on criterion of interest → back to summary → zoom on next criterion → summary for decision call.

## Supported Candidate Operations

### Op-1: Assertion Extraction (AE)
**Rule**: For each content chunk (paragraph, section, or logical unit), extract exactly one full-sentence assertion. That assertion becomes the slide headline. The slide body contains only the visual evidence supporting that assertion.
**Trigger**: Any slide with >50 words of body text, or any slide whose title is a topic label (noun phrase) rather than a claim (full sentence).
**Parameters**: assertion (string, required), evidence_visual (type: chart | image | key_number | icon | diagram, required), supporting_detail (string, optional, goes to speaker notes).

### Op-2: Conjunction Split (CS)
**Rule**: Scan every slide headline for the conjunctions "and," "or," "but," "however," "while," "although." If found, split into N slides where N = number of independent clauses connected by conjunctions.
**Trigger**: Any slide headline containing coordinating or subordinating conjunctions that connect two independent claims.
**Parameters**: original_headline (string, required), split_assertions (list[string], required).

### Op-3: Density Compliance Check (DCC)
**Rule**: Apply the 5-5-5 rule as a quantitative gate. Body text ≤ 5 bullet points, each ≤ 5 words. If violated, flag the slide and trigger Assertion Extraction or Conjunction Split. Additionally, count consecutive text-heavy slides and insert a visual-only "reset" slide after every 5th text-heavy slide.
**Trigger**: Every slide during quality review pass.
**Parameters**: bullet_count (int, max 5), words_per_bullet (int, max 5), consecutive_text_slides (int, max 5).

### Op-4: Squint Test Verification (STV)
**Rule**: At reduced visual acuity (simulated or actual 3m distance), the slide must have exactly one discernible focal point. If multiple competing elements are visible, split until the test passes.
**Trigger**: After initial slide design, before finalization.
**Parameters**: focal_point_count (int, must be 1), competing_elements (list[string], items to split out).

### Op-5: Pyramid Compression with Appendix Fallback (PCAF)
**Rule**: When slide count is externally constrained to N, restructure content into a 3-tier architecture: (a) Core N slides using Pyramid Principle (answer first, then evidence), (b) Appendix with uncapped full-detail slides, (c) Backup "trigger slides" mapped to likely audience questions.
**Trigger**: External constraint "max N slides" where N is less than the number of distinct ideas.
**Parameters**: max_slides (int, required), core_assertion (string, required), supporting_evidence (list[string], required), appendix_slides (list, optional), trigger_slides (list, optional).

## Rejected or Weak Candidate Operations

### Rejected-1: Pure Word Count Threshold (e.g., "max 30 words per slide, no exceptions")
**Reason for rejection**: A 30-word slide containing a 4x4 comparison matrix at 8pt font is objectively worse than a 50-word slide with a single large chart and three bullet points. Word count alone fails to capture visual complexity, font size, or information architecture. The metric is directionally correct but insufficient as a standalone rule. The Density Compliance Check (Op-3) subsumes word count within a richer framework (bullet count + words per bullet + consecutive text slide tracker + visual variety requirement).

### Rejected-2: Fixed Slide-to-Minute Ratio (e.g., "1 slide = 2 minutes, so a 20-minute talk = 10 slides")
**Reason for rejection**: Slide duration varies enormously by content type. A full-bleed photograph may occupy 15 seconds while a complex diagram with build animation may require 4 minutes. A "one idea" slide can resolve in 10 seconds if the idea is simple (e.g., "Revenue grew 15%"). The ratio is a useful heuristic for keynote-style talks but breaks down for technical, educational, or data-dense presentations. The Pyramid Compression operation (Op-5) provides a more robust alternative by prioritizing content architecture over mechanical ratio calculation.

## Boundaries and Uncertainties

1. **Cultural variance in information density tolerance**: The 5-5-5 and 6x6 rules originate from Western (primarily US) business presentation culture. East Asian academic and business presentation norms (e.g., Japan, China, Korea) often tolerate significantly higher text density on slides. A universal rule may not apply; the skill should include a "density calibration" step based on audience cultural context.

2. **Domain-specific exceptions**: Scientific conference presentations (posters and slides) routinely violate the 5-5-5 rule because the audience expects to read detailed methodology. Medical grand rounds similarly use dense slides as reference documents. The operational rules above may need a "domain override" flag for academic, medical, and legal contexts where slides serve dual purposes (presentation + archival record).

3. **Automation ceiling**: While assertion extraction from well-structured prose is feasible via LLM, the quality of extraction depends heavily on source document structure. A poorly written source document (circular arguments, buried ledes, implicit claims) will produce poor assertion extractions. The skill should clearly communicate this "garbage in, garbage out" constraint to users.

4. **Animation-free environments**: Several decomposition techniques (progressive disclosure, build animations, layered reveals) depend on animation support. In environments where animation is disabled or unsupported (e.g., printed decks, certain corporate templates, PDF exports), these techniques fail. The skill needs animation-free fallback patterns (e.g., multi-slide builds instead of single-slide reveals).

5. **The "slideument" boundary is fuzzy**: The distinction between "presentation" and "document" is a spectrum, not a binary. Some organizational cultures genuinely require slides to function as both simultaneously. The skill's ability to negotiate this boundary (appendix strategy, notes view export, separate handout) may be constrained by organizational IT policies and document management systems.

## Recommendations for Later Skill Compilation

1. **Integrate R2 findings with R1 (audience analysis)**: Density rules should be modulated by audience type. A technical review committee has different density tolerance than a keynote audience. The skill should chain: R1 identifies audience → R2 selects density parameters.

2. **Build an "Assertion Extraction" LLM prompt template**: This is the highest-leverage single operation. A well-engineered prompt that takes a paragraph and returns (assertion_headline, evidence_type, supporting_detail) would automate the bottleneck step in decomposition. Test against edge cases: multi-claim paragraphs, paragraphs with implicit claims, paragraphs with claims buried in subordinate clauses.

3. **Create a quantitative "Density Score" metric**: Combine bullet count, words per bullet, font size, visual element count, and slide-to-assertion ratio into a single numeric score (0-100) that triggers decomposition recommendations. The arxiv paper (2508.19289) on automated slide quality assessment provides a methodological starting point.

4. **Define animation-free decomposition patterns explicitly**: Since many enterprise environments disable animations, the skill needs a documented set of "multi-slide build" patterns that achieve progressive disclosure through slide sequencing rather than on-slide animations.

5. **Develop "stakeholder negotiation scripts"**: The dilemma cases reveal that decomposition is often a persuasion problem, not a design problem. The skill should include ready-to-use communication scripts for common objections: "More slides means longer presentation," "I need everything on one slide for the handout," "My boss won't accept more than N slides," "The audience expects detailed slides in our industry."

6. **Specify integration with slide generation tools**: The decomposition output (list of {assertion, evidence_type, notes}) is a structured intermediate representation. Document the expected input format for downstream slide generation tools (e.g., python-pptx, Google Slides API, reveal.js) so the skill can serve as a pipeline stage.
