## Key Findings

### KF1: SCQA and Pyramid Principle are complementary, not competing
SCQA (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer) and the Pyramid Principle were both created by Barbara Minto and are designed to work in sequence: SCQA opens the presentation (building engagement and shared reality), then the Pyramid Principle structures the main argument body (conclusion-first, MECE logic). The dominant consulting firm pattern is: SCQA opening hook (30-60s) -> Core Message (Pyramid top) -> Supporting Arguments (Pyramid body) -> SCQA recap with call to action. They are not an either/or choice -- they solve different problems at different points in the same presentation.

### KF2: Nancy Duarte's Sparkline is the universal emotional rhythm across all great presentations
Duarte analyzed both Steve Jobs' iPhone 2007 keynote and MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech and found an identical structural pattern: oscillating between "what is" (reality/problem) and "what could be" (ideal/solution), with each oscillation drilling deeper into both the pain of the present and the promise of the future, culminating in "New Bliss." This Sparkline pattern -- not bullet points, not data density -- is what makes audiences lean in, remember, and share. One contrast is forgettable; repeated contrasts build irresistible momentum. The golden rule: "The thrill isn't at the height -- it's in the drop. The audience is moved not by 'always good,' but by 'nearly destroyed yet alive.'"

### KF3: Audience type determines which ordering of the SCQA elements to use, not whether to use SCQA at all
SCQA has four ordering variants that map to audience disposition: Standard (S-C-Q-A) for neutral/unfamiliar audiences; Answer-first (A-S-C) for impatient C-suite executives; Complication-first (C-S-Q-A) for crisis situations requiring urgency; Question-first (Q-S-C-A) for curious, engaged audiences needing an intellectual hook. The SCQA sequence is not fixed -- it is a tuning knob controlled by audience readiness and time pressure.

### KF4: Apple Keynote vs TED Talk represent two poles of the same hero-positioning axis
The core structural difference is not "narrative vs. no-narrative" but who plays the role of the hero. In Apple keynotes, the product (or the audience liberated by it) is the hero and the presenter is the visionary guide unveiling a new world. In TED talks, the audience is the hero and the speaker models a personal journey of transformation as the mentor (Duarte's rule: "You're not Luke Skywalker, you're Yoda"). Both use the same Sparkline rhythm and the same three-act dramatic arc (Problem -> Solution -> Resolution), but the hero positioning shifts everything downstream: slide design, pacing, data-to-story ratio, and the emotional climax.

### KF5: Three yes/no questions determine the entire narrative skeleton
A remarkably simple decision tree emerges from the research: (1) Does my audience already agree there is a problem? (2) How much time do I have? (3) Am I informing or persuading? The answers to these three questions cascade into every subsequent structural choice: whether to lead with answer (pyramid) or context (SCQA), whether to use a full narrative arc or a compressed one, and whether to position the presenter as guide or hero. Across all presentation types -- C-suite decision briefs, academic defenses, product pitches, and weekly status reports -- the same three questions hold.

## Dilemma Decision Cases (>=2 required)

### Case 1: 老板要结论先行(Pyramid) vs 这个故事需要悬念(SCQA)
- 困境 (Dilemma): CEO demands a "bottom-line-up-front" presentation (answer first, no storytelling buildup). But the proposal involves a controversial organizational restructure that will provoke emotional resistance. If you lead with the recommendation, the audience will reject it before understanding the rationale. If you use SCQA buildup, the CEO will interrupt: "Just tell me what you want."
- 约束 (Constraints): 15-minute slot. Mixed audience of CEO (impatient) and department heads (skeptical). One chance to get approval. CEO has explicitly stated "I hate storytelling in business presentations."
- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. Recognize that "answer-first" does not mean "context-free." The Pyramid Principle allows you to state the answer, then immediately provide the governing thought (because/so that...).
  2. Use the A-S-C variant of SCQA: Answer first ("We need to restructure X division"), then Situation ("Here's where we are"), then Complication ("...which is why this will feel painful, and here's why it's necessary").
  3. Embed the "why this matters" emotional hook inside the supporting arguments, not in the opening. Each supporting point follows a micro-SCQA (mini complication -> mini resolution).
  4. End with a forward-looking vision (the Sparkline "New Bliss") to address the emotional resistance that the opening bypassed.
- 结果 (Outcome): The CEO gets his answer in the first 30 seconds and stays engaged. Department heads, who needed the emotional buildup, get it distributed across the body as micro-narratives inside each argument pillar. The presentation satisfies both cognitive styles without violating either.
- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): **Answer-first opening + nested micro-SCQA body**. When audience demands pyramid but topic demands narrative, invert the SCQA: lead with the answer, then distribute the emotional buildup as micro-stories inside each supporting argument. This satisfies the time-poor decision-maker while still building buy-in with skeptics.

### Case 2: 内容复杂 vs 时间极短 (5分钟 vs 30分钟) -- 叙事颗粒度怎么调
- 困境 (Dilemma): You have 30 minutes of substantive analysis -- market data, competitive landscape, financial projections, risk assessment -- but only a 5-minute speaking slot. You cannot simply "talk faster" or "cut slides." You need to decide what to preserve and what to sacrifice without losing persuasiveness.
- 约束 (Constraints): 5 minutes strict. Audience is a steering committee that will make a go/no-go decision based on this presentation. They have not read any pre-read materials. The data is genuinely complex -- this is not an oversimplification problem.
- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. Identify the single governing thought (the one sentence that must land). Everything else is negotiable.
  2. Map the full 30-minute SCQA+Pyramid structure on paper first. Then compress each SCQA element into its minimum viable form: S = one sentence, C = one sentence, Q = one rhetorical question, A = one sentence. This is a 4-sentence narrative skeleton.
  3. Choose ONE data point (not three, not five) that makes the Complication undeniable. The goal is not to prove with evidence but to trigger intuitive recognition ("oh, I see the problem now").
  4. Use the Sparkline instead of the Pyramid for the body: a single oscillation between "this is the painful reality" and "this is what we could achieve." Skip the three supporting arguments. The single contrast is the argument.
  5. End with exactly one ask. Not three recommendations, not two options. One.
- 结果 (Outcome): The presentation is reduced from a 30-minute evidence-heavy argument to a 5-minute emotional-logic arc: one shared reality, one painful truth, one vision, one ask. Steering committee members report feeling the presentation was "complete" even though 85% of the data was cut. The governing thought landed because nothing competed with it.
- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): **The "One of Everything" Rule (1-1-1-1-1 Rule)**. For ultra-short presentations: One Situation sentence, one Complication sentence, one Question, one Answer sentence, one data point, one vision, one ask. When time collapses, compress all structural elements to their minimum viable singularity -- do not collapse the structure itself.

### Case 3: 学术答辩(严谨性优先) vs 产品发布会(情感冲击优先) -- 同一套数据,两种叙事
- 困境 (Dilemma): A research lab is presenting breakthrough results. The same data needs to be presented in two venues in the same week: first to a thesis defense committee (who will challenge every methodological choice), then to an industry investor pitch (who only cares about commercial potential). The data is identical. The structure cannot be identical. How do you rebuild the narrative skeleton without fabricating or oversimplifying the science?
- 约束 (Constraints): Same presenter, same core data, same week. Committee expects methodological rigor and acknowledges limitations. Investors expect bold claims and a clear path to market. The presenter cannot contradict herself between the two audiences.
- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. Identify the invariant core: the governing thought and the key evidence chain. This must be identical across both presentations -- the ground truth.
  2. For the defense: Use Pyramid Principle as the dominant body structure (conclusion -> evidence group 1 -> evidence group 2 -> evidence group 3), with SCQA opening only to frame the research gap. Hero = the research process. Presenter = humble investigator. Embrace the "limitations" section as a credibility signal.
  3. For the pitch: Use full SCQA + Sparkline. Reframe the same governing thought as a "what is -> what could be" journey. The Complication becomes the market pain, not the methodological gap. Hero = the audience liberated by this technology. Presenter = visionary guide. Convert the "limitations" section into "future roadmap" -- same content, different framing.
  4. Create a "framing translator" -- a one-page mapping that shows how each academic finding maps to a commercial benefit, ensuring the presenter never contradicts herself.
- 结果 (Outcome): The defense committee approves the thesis with distinction (methodological rigor satisfied). The investors express strong interest (emotional resonance landed). No contradiction exists between the two presentations -- they are different framings of the same truth, each optimized for audience epistemology.
- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): **Audience Epistemology Mapping**. Before designing any narrative, answer: "How does this audience determine what is true?" Academics: methodological transparency + limitation acknowledgment = credibility. C-suite: clarity + actionable recommendation = credibility. Investors: vision + market logic = credibility. Match the narrative structure to the audience's epistemology, not to the presenter's comfort zone.

## Supported Candidate Operations

### CO1: SCQA Ordering Selector (audience-readiness-based)
**Rule**: Select the SCQA element order based on two axes -- audience agreement on the problem (high/low) and time pressure (high/low).
- Low agreement + low time pressure -> S-C-Q-A (Standard)
- Low agreement + high time pressure -> C-S-Q-A (Complication-first, create urgency fast)
- High agreement + low time pressure -> Q-S-C-A (Question-first, engage intellectually)
- High agreement + high time pressure -> A-S-C (Answer-first, inverted pyramid)
**Source**: KF3, Case 1

### CO2: The "One of Everything" Compression Rule (1-1-1-1-1)
**Rule**: When presentation time is <=5 minutes, compress the narrative to exactly one instance of each structural element: one Situation, one Complication, one Question, one data point, one vision, one ask. Never collapse the structure itself -- only compress each element to its minimum viable form. The structure is the skeleton; do not remove bones.
**Source**: KF5, Case 2

### CO3: Hero Positioning Decision (Guide vs. Protagonist)
**Rule**: Before designing the narrative arc, decide who is the hero:
- If the audience needs to take action -> audience is the hero, presenter is Yoda/guide (TED model, change management, pitches)
- If a product/innovation/research is being unveiled -> product/innovation is the hero, presenter is the visionary unveiling it (Apple model, product launches)
- If the presenter's credibility is being evaluated -> research process is the hero, presenter is the humble investigator (academic defense model)
**Source**: KF4, Case 3

### CO4: Audience Epistemology Mapper
**Rule**: For any given presentation, first answer: "How does this audience determine what is true?" Then match the narrative structure to that epistemology:
- Academics: methodological transparency + limitation acknowledgment -> Pyramid body with explicit limitations section
- C-suite: clarity + actionable bottom line -> Answer-first SCQA variant
- Investors: vision + market logic -> Sparkline with commercial framing
- Internal team: operational specificity + honest status -> Pyramid for status, SCQA micro-narrative for escalations
**Source**: KF3, KF5, Case 3

### CO5: The Three-Question Narrative Decision Tree
**Rule**: Before any structural design, answer these three questions in sequence. The answers cascade deterministically into the narrative skeleton:
1. Does my audience already agree there is a problem? (No -> SCQA opening needed; Yes -> Pyramid/conclusion-first)
2. How much time do I have? (<5 min -> 1-1-1-1-1 Rule; 5-15 min -> Sparkline only, no pyramid sub-arguments; 15+ min -> full SCQA + Pyramid + close)
3. Am I informing or persuading? (Informing -> Pyramid body dominant; Persuading -> Sparkline dominant)
**Source**: KF5, Case 1, Case 2

## Rejected or Weak Candidate Operations

### RW1: "Always start with a story"
**Why rejected**: While storytelling is powerful, it is not universal. C-suite audiences with <3 minutes and high alignment on the problem actively resent being "story-ed to." The correct rule is "start with what your audience needs to hear first" -- which is audience-conditional, not a universal. This "always-story" advice failed the audience-epistemology test in Case 1 and Case 3.

### RW2: "Use the Hero's Journey for every presentation"
**Why rejected**: The monomyth (Call to Adventure -> Trials -> Transformation -> Return) is a feature-length narrative arc. Attempting to compress it into a 5-minute presentation produces a cartoon version that undermines credibility. More importantly, not every presentation has a hero. Status updates, compliance reports, and routine operational reviews have no dramatic arc -- forcing one reads as insincere. The Hero's Journey is a tool for transformation narratives, not a universal template.

## Boundaries and Uncertainties

1. **Cultural variability of SCQA**: The SCQA framework was developed in a Western consulting context where directness is valued. The ordering variants (especially Answer-first) may not transfer cleanly to high-context cultures (e.g., Japan, Korea) where relationship-building and indirect communication are the norm. This research did not explore cross-cultural narrative adaptation.

2. **Sparkline generalizability beyond English-language presentations**: Duarte's Sparkline research analyzed English-language speeches (Jobs, MLK). The emotional oscillation pattern may vary across cultures with different rhetorical traditions. Chinese or Japanese presentations, for instance, often use a "ki-sho-ten-ketsu" (起承转合) structure that does not map cleanly to the Sparkline.

3. **Audience epistemology is a hypothesis, not a validated taxonomy**: The four-category audience epistemology mapping (academic/c-suite/investor/internal) is derived from pattern observation across sources, not from empirical studies. There may be edge cases (e.g., a C-suite executive with an academic background, a venture capitalist who was formerly an engineer) that cross categories.

4. **Time compression limits**: The 1-1-1-1-1 Rule has a theoretical floor. Below approximately 90 seconds (elevator pitch territory), even the minimum-viable structure may collapse, requiring a different framework altogether. The boundary between "compressed presentation" and "elevator pitch" needs further definition.

5. **Multi-modal presentations (slide + oral + handout)**: This research focused on the oral/slide narrative. In practice, presentations are increasingly multi-modal (pre-read deck, live talk, follow-up handout). The narrative structure may need to be distributed across modalities rather than concentrated in the live talk. This was not explored.

## Recommendations for Later Skill Compilation

1. **Build the Three-Question Decision Tree as a structured entry point**: The most reliable finding across this research is that three questions cascade deterministically into structural choices. This should be the first thing the skill asks the user when invoked -- before any design work begins.

2. **Create a "Framing Translator" template for multi-audience scenarios** (from Case 3): A standardized mapping that converts academic framing to commercial framing, internal framing to executive framing, etc., without altering the underlying truth. This is a reusable deliverable.

3. **Define "Presentation Archetype Cards" as a quick-reference**: Four archetypes (Executive Brief, Academic Defense, Product Pitch, Status Report) each with a pre-validated structural recipe (SCQA ordering, hero positioning, body structure, close type). This reduces cognitive load during skill execution.

4. **Integrate with Nancy Duarte's Sparkline as a visual planning tool**: The Sparkline (oscillating line between "what is" and "what could be") is inherently visual and could be implemented as a planning canvas or diagram within the skill workflow, used to storyboard the emotional arc before slides are built.

5. **Research gap: cross-cultural narrative patterns**: Before the skill is finalized, conduct research on non-Western narrative structures (ki-sho-ten-ketsu, Chinese "cheng-zhuan-qi-he", etc.) and their compatibility with SCQA/Pyramid/Sparkline, to ensure the skill is not culturally bounded.

6. **Define the "time-to-structure" mapping explicitly**: Create a table that maps presentation duration (90s, 3min, 5min, 10min, 20min, 45min) to the maximum viable structural complexity at each tier. This would operationalize the 1-1-1-1-1 Rule into a full gradient.
