## Key Findings

### 1. Duarte's Sparkline creates emotional rhythm through deliberate "What Is vs. What Could Be" oscillation
Nancy Duarte's Persuasive Presentation Form (Sparkline) maps a presentation as an up-and-down waveform oscillating between "What Is" (current unsatisfactory reality) and "What Could Be" (aspirational vision). The rhythm is intentional: begin grounded in "What Is," repeatedly toggle between the two states in the middle (creating tension and release), and land firmly in "What Could Be" at the end (the "New Bliss"). Flat presentations that spend too long in "What Is" and only briefly mention vision at the end bore audiences. The gap between the two states creates urgency and emotional investment — audiences begin to find the status quo unacceptable.

### 2. Kawasaki's 10/20/30 Rule: three mutually reinforcing constraints produce discipline
Guy Kawasaki's rule — 10 slides maximum, 20 minutes delivery, 30-point minimum font — works because the constraints reinforce each other. Ten slides forces editorial discipline (a normal human cannot comprehend more than 10 concepts in one meeting). Twenty minutes aligns with the human attention span ceiling and leaves 40 minutes for discussion in a 60-minute slot. Thirty-point font prevents slides from becoming scripts — the audience reads faster than you speak, so text-heavy slides create sync loss. The speaker becomes the main event, not the deck.

### 3. TED-calibrated pacing: ~1 slide per minute, ~180-220 words per minute, 18-minute cap with 3-part structural ratio
TED's 18-minute cap is based on attention-span research. Professional speakers target roughly one slide per minute as a baseline cadence (Nancy Duarte recommends cutting at least half the slides you would use in a longer talk). Optimal speech rate is 180-220 effective words per minute — faster and audiences disengage, slower and energy flags. The three-act structural ratio is approximately 20% setup / 60% argument / 20% conclusion. Audience attention peaks at roughly minute 7 and in the final 5 minutes (dopamine cycle research). Duarte recommends embedding countdown benchmarks at 6, 12, and 18 minutes so the speaker always knows where they should be.

### 4. Rehearsal discipline: "hour per minute" rule, two natural endings, and pre-planned cuttable sections
Nancy Duarte's benchmark: for an 18-minute talk, budget roughly 18 hours of rehearsal (the "hour-per-minute" principle; less experienced speakers may need more). Rehearsal time is inversely proportional to talk length — the shorter the talk, the more you rehearse. Critical rehearsal tactics include: (a) videotaping rehearsals to review presence, (b) practicing with clock counting up to measure total runtime, (c) practicing with clock counting down to internalize checkpoint pacing, (d) designing two natural ending points — one at ~85% of allotted time, one at 100% — so if the speaker before you goes long or you run over, you can still end gracefully without appearing to have been cut off.

### 5. Trimming priority when overtime: cut from the outside in, never sacrifice the single core message
When rehearsal runs long, the consistent expert consensus is: never speed up your speaking pace (audiences cannot "speed learn"), and never cut your core message or call to action. Instead, trim using the **layering** (onion) approach — peel off outer layers (tangential examples, extra proof points, excessive background) while protecting the inner core (one key message + strongest supporting point + call to action). An alternative is **modularizing** — structure content as independent "modules" that can be dropped without breaking narrative continuity. It is better to fully deliver one compelling point than to race through three. Proactive strategy: plan 10-15% less content than you think you need, because presenters inevitably run long.

## Dilemma Decision Cases (≥2 required)

### Case 1: Rehearsal Running 5 Minutes Over — Cut Final Slides vs. Preserve Core Argument
- **困境 (Dilemma):** You have an 18-minute TED-format talk. In full rehearsal, you clock 23 minutes — 5 minutes over. Your final 3 slides contain your strongest supporting data, your emotional closing story, and your call to action. The temptation is to cut the last 3 slides (the "back end trim"). But doing so drops your payoff. The alternative is to identify and trim from the middle — but that requires reworking transitional logic and may weaken the argument chain.

- **约束 (Constraints):** 18-minute hard cap enforced by the event organizer. Slides are already built and sequenced. No time to rebuild the deck from scratch (rehearsal is tomorrow). Audience expectation has been set by the abstract/promo for specific content.

- **决策步骤 (Decision Steps):**
  1. **Mark every section as "core" (must-keep) or "expendable" (can-drop).** Core = thesis statement, strongest piece of evidence, call to action. Expendable = second/third examples, background the audience already knows, any content that "would be nice" but is not essential to the argument.
  2. **Apply the layering trim, not the back-end chop.** Start from the middle: cut one redundant example, collapse two supporting points into one, reduce the setup/background by 30 seconds. Do not touch the final 3 slides.
  3. **Use countdown benchmarks to locate the bloat.** If the 6-minute checkpoint arrives at minute 8 in rehearsal, the bloat is in the first act — trim setup, not conclusion. If the 12-minute checkpoint arrives at minute 15, the bloat is in the middle — consolidate the argument section.
  4. **If the bloat is evenly distributed, cut the weakest module whole.** Dropping one complete module is cleaner than shaving 20 seconds from five different sections (which produces a rushed, breathless feel).
  5. **Re-rehearse the trimmed version with the clock counting down.** Verify that the core message and emotional close remain intact.

- **结果 (Outcome):** The talk lands at 17:30 — under the cap with a 30-second buffer. The argument spine (thesis → evidence → call to action) is preserved intact. One secondary example was dropped, but the audience never missed it because the primary example was strong enough to carry the point alone.

- **可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation):** **"Core-First Triage"** — Before any rehearsal, tag every content segment as CORE (cannot be removed without breaking the argument) or EXPENDABLE (can be dropped while preserving the message). When overtime hits, cut from expendable only. Never cut from the back end by default — the close and call to action are always CORE. Use countdown benchmarks (at 25%, 50%, 75% of total time) to locate exactly where the bloat lives, then cut surgically rather than amputating the ending.

### Case 2: Boss Says "15 Minutes" but Content Needs 25 — What to Cut, What Must Stay
- **困境 (Dilemma):** You have prepared a comprehensive 25-minute business presentation covering: problem background (5 min), market analysis/data (6 min), proposed solution (6 min), implementation plan (3 min), risk assessment (2 min), Q&A setup and call to action (3 min). Your boss says: "Keep it to 15 minutes, we have a tight agenda." You cannot simply "talk faster" — the content genuinely requires explanation. Cutting 10 minutes (40% of the talk) means making hard choices about what the audience does and does not hear.

- **约束 (Constraints):** 15-minute hard limit (agenda-driven, not negotiable). Audience includes senior decision-makers who need enough context to make a judgment, but who will disengage if you go long. You cannot send a pre-read document (the meeting culture does not support it). The boss is evaluating your ability to be concise under pressure.

- **决策步骤 (Decision Steps):**
  1. **Apply Kawasaki's 10-slide gate check.** Can the entire narrative fit on 10 slides? If not, you are trying to convey too many concepts. Reduce to 10 slides first — this forces you to eliminate whole concepts rather than shaving sentences.
  2. **Identify the single irreducible question the audience needs answered.** For a decision-making meeting, this is: "What should we do and why?" Everything that does not directly answer this question is negotiable.
  3. **Apply the "One Breath Test" to the core message.** Can you state the central argument in the time it takes to exhale one breath (~15 seconds)? If not, the message is not crisp enough. Refine until it passes.
  4. **Build the 15-minute version as a standalone talk, not a truncated 25-minute talk.** Starting from scratch with the 15-minute constraint produces a fundamentally different (and better) structure than trying to compress. The 25-minute talk was built for a different purpose.
  5. **Ruthless content triage:**
     - **KEEP (non-negotiable):** Problem statement (1 min), proposed solution (3 min), why this solution works — strongest evidence only (3 min), what you need from the audience / call to action (1 min), and 2 minutes buffer for questions embedded throughout.
     - **CUT:** Detailed market analysis (shift to a one-sentence assertion with an offer to "provide the full data as a follow-up"), implementation plan details (shift to "we have a detailed roadmap ready for review"), risk assessment (keep only the top risk — one sentence — and how you are mitigating it).
  6. **Build an appendix of "deferred content"** — slides placed after the "Thank You" slide that answer anticipated questions. If asked, you flip to them. This offloads content from the main talk without losing it.

- **结果 (Outcome):** The 15-minute version delivers a crisp, confident argument: "Here is the problem, here is our solution, here is why it works, here is what we need from you." The presentation feels more authoritative (not rushed), the audience asks better questions because the argument is clear, and the deferred-content appendix allows you to demonstrate depth without bloating the talk. The boss is satisfied.

- **可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation):** **"Constraint-First Redesign"** — When a time cut exceeds 30% of original length, do not attempt to compress the existing talk. Instead, rebuild from the constraint outward: (a) state the irreducible core message in one breath, (b) identify what the audience must know to act (vs. what they might find interesting), (c) build the shortest possible version that answers the decision question, (d) move everything else to a deferred-content appendix accessible on demand. The talk that is built for 15 minutes is always better than the talk that was trimmed from 25 minutes.

### Case 3: The Speaker Before You Runs 10 Minutes Over — Your 20-Minute Slot Becomes 10 Minutes
- **困境 (Dilemma):** You are third in a three-speaker panel. The first two speakers each ran 5 minutes over. You now have 10 minutes for a talk you prepared and rehearsed for 20 minutes. You have 60 seconds before you take the stage to decide what to do. If you try to deliver all your slides at double speed, you will sound frantic and lose the audience. If you cut half your content on the fly without a plan, you risk incoherence.

- **约束 (Constraints):** Real-time decision with no preparation window. The slides are loaded and cannot be reordered. The audience is tired from two long talks and has diminished attention. You must respect the event's end time (the room turnover crew is waiting).

- **决策步骤 (Decision Steps):**
  1. **Activate the pre-planned "short version" ending.** Having a second natural ending point (designed in rehearsal, per Duarte's recommendation) means you know exactly which slides to jump to for a clean close — without appearing panicked or cut off.
  2. **Skip to the core argument and call to action immediately.** Audiences remember the last thing they hear most vividly (recency effect). Your job in 10 minutes is to deliver your core message and a clear next step, nothing more.
  3. **Verbally acknowledge the situation with confidence, not apology:** "We have less time than planned, so I am going to give you the heart of what I came to share." This signals competence and respect for the audience's time.
  4. **Offer the full deck as a follow-up resource.** This converts a negative situation into a relationship-building opportunity.

- **结果 (Outcome):** The audience receives a tight, memorable 10-minute talk that feels intentional rather than butchered. The speaker looks professional and adaptable. The full deck is emailed afterward, generating follow-up conversations.

- **可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation):** **"Two-Ending Safety Net"** — During rehearsal, always design and practice a complete second ending that lands at approximately 50-60% of your allotted time. This ending must include your core message and call to action. When real-time time compression hits (common in panels, conferences, all-hands meetings), you activate the short ending without hesitation. You do not rush. You do not apologize excessively. You deliver the short version as if it was always the plan.

## Supported Candidate Operations

### Op 1: Sparkline Rhythm Audit
Map the presentation onto Duarte's "What Is vs. What Could Be" waveform. Verify that the oscillation frequency is no less than once every 5 minutes (a talk that stays in "What Is" for too long loses audience engagement). Ensure the talk ends on "What Could Be" (New Bliss + Call to Action). If the waveform is flat (mostly "What Is" with a brief vision at the end), restructure to create intentional contrast rhythm.

### Op 2: 10/20/30 Gate Check
Before any rehearsal, audit the draft against Kawasaki's three constraints: (a) Does the talk fit on 10 slides or fewer? If not, consolidate concepts until it does. (b) Can the full talk be delivered in 20 minutes? If not, identify what to cut before rehearsing. (c) Is the minimum font size 30 points on every slide? If not, distill text until it is. These three checks are applied as a gating criterion — a talk that fails any one of them is not ready for rehearsal.

### Op 3: Time-Benchmarked Structure with Built-In Trim Points
Divide the talk into time-benchmarked segments at 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% of total allotment. For each segment, tag content as CORE (must-survive) or EXPENDABLE (can-drop). During rehearsal, use countdown timers to verify that each checkpoint is hit. If a checkpoint is missed, trim from expendable content in the next segment. Additionally, design TWO natural endings: Ending A at ~85% of time (full message, shortened close) and Ending B at 100% of time (full message, full close). Practice both.

### Op 4: Constraint-First Redesign (for ≥30% Time Compression)
When the allotted time is cut by 30% or more, do not compress the existing talk. Instead: (a) state the irreducible core message in one breath (~15 seconds), (b) identify what the audience must know to act vs. what is merely interesting, (c) build the shortest possible version from scratch that answers the decision question, (d) move all non-essential content to a deferred-content appendix (slides placed after the closing slide, accessible only if questions arise). The rebuilt talk is always superior to the truncated original.

### Op 5: Rehearsal-to-Runtime Ratio Calculator
Apply the "hour per minute" heuristic adjustably: for experienced speakers, budget 30-45 minutes of rehearsal per minute of talk; for less experienced speakers, budget 60-90 minutes. More critically, ensure rehearsal includes: (a) one full run with clock counting UP (measure total), (b) one full run with clock counting DOWN (internalize checkpoints), (c) at least one video-recorded run (review presence, pace, filler words), (d) at least one run with a "brutally honest" external reviewer present. The rehearsal budget should be front-loaded into the project timeline, not treated as an afterthought.

## Rejected or Weak Candidate Operations

### Rejected: "One Slide Per Minute" as a Strict Rule
While widely cited (especially in TED-adjacent coaching), "one slide per minute" breaks down in practice for many presentation types. Data-heavy slides (charts, diagrams) may require 2-3 minutes of explanation. Visual-storytelling slides (full-bleed images with no text) may pass in 15 seconds. A rigid slide-per-minute rule encourages either rushing through complex content or padding simple slides with filler. The better rule: each slide should take exactly as long as its content demands, and the total runtime (not slide count) is the binding constraint. If a slide takes 3 minutes, that is fine — as long as the 10-slide and 20-minute caps are respected.

### Rejected: "Just Talk Faster" as an Overtime Remedy
The worst advice in presentation coaching. Research consistently shows that when speech rate exceeds ~220 words per minute, comprehension drops sharply — audiences cannot "speed learn." Cognitive load increases, retention plummets, and the speaker appears nervous or disrespectful. The correct remedy for overtime is always to cut content, never to accelerate delivery. If you must cut on the fly, drop an entire section and deliver the remainder at a calm, deliberate pace. The audience will not know what they missed, but they will absolutely notice if you sound frantic.

## Boundaries and Uncertainties

1. **Cultural variance in speech rate norms.** The 180-220 wpm optimal range is derived from English-language research (primarily North American audiences). Speech rate expectations vary by language and culture — some cultures expect faster, higher-energy delivery; others expect slower, more deliberate pacing. The skill should flag that these numbers are defaults requiring cultural calibration.

2. **10/20/30 rule applicability outside pitch contexts.** Kawasaki's 10-slide structure is explicitly designed for venture capital pitches. While the principles generalize (constraints breed clarity), the specific 10-slide template (Problem, Solution, Business Model, etc.) does not directly transfer to, e.g., academic lectures, keynote speeches, or internal project updates. The skill should offer domain-specific adaptations rather than presenting the VC slide template as universal.

3. **"Hour per minute" rehearsal ratio is aspirational, not always practical.** In corporate settings, speakers rarely have 18 hours to rehearse a presentation. The heuristic is useful as a north star (more rehearsal = better talk) but the skill should offer scaled-down rehearsal protocols for time-constrained professionals (e.g., "if you only have 3 hours, here is what to prioritize").

4. **Sparkline rhythm may not suit all presentation types.** Duarte's "What Is vs. What Could Be" oscillation is optimized for persuasive talks (pitches, keynotes, vision presentations). For informational/educational presentations (training, status reports, technical walkthroughs), the emotional-contrast structure may feel forced or manipulative. The skill should distinguish between persuasive-rhythm structures and explanatory-rhythm structures.

5. **Two-ending strategy requires disclosure awareness.** In some high-stakes contexts (investor pitches, board presentations), visibly "skipping" slides can signal poor preparation or disrespect for the audience. The two-ending strategy works best when the short ending is practiced to be indistinguishable from the intended talk — the audience should not perceive that anything was cut.
