# Research Notes — PPT Architect

## Evidence Summary

This skill is distilled from 50+ academic and professional sources across presentation design, information design, cognitive psychology, and data visualization.

### Pillar 1: Narrative Architecture

Minto's Pyramid Principle, McKinsey's SCQA framework, and Duarte's Sparkline model form the three primary narrative structures. The key operational insight: these are complementary, not competing — Pyramid for executive reporting, SCQA for persuasive proposals, Sparkline for visionary keynotes. The Three-Question Decision Tree (R1) provides the routing logic.

### Pillar 2: Slide Decomposition

Research (R2) converges on concrete numerical rules: 1-6-6 (one idea, six bullets, six words each), 5-5-5 (five words per line, five lines, five text-heavy slides in a row max), and Kawasaki's 30pt minimum. The deeper principle from Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller): extraneous load from dense slides directly reduces learning.

### Pillar 3: Visual Design for Non-Designers

Robin Williams' CRAP principles (Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity) and research on typographic hierarchy (R3) provide decision rules that don't require design training. 60-30-10 color allocation, 3-level typographic scale with golden ratio spacing, and 65% content area maximum are the core heuristics.

### Pillar 4: Data Visualization for Projection

Tufte's data-ink ratio and Few's chart selection framework are adapted for projection with higher contrast requirements and larger minimum font sizes. The key finding (R4): Excel default charts are universally unsuitable for projection and require a structured "Rescue Protocol."

### Pillar 5: Rhythm & Time

Duarte's Sparkline oscillation pattern, Kawasaki's 10/20/30 rule, and TED's 18-minute constraint all converge on the same finding: attention decays after ~10 minutes requiring format switches. The Onion Trim model (R5) provides a principled approach to time compression that protects the core argument.

### Pillar 6: Anti-Pattern Detection

The 20-item anti-pattern checklist (R6) is grounded in Mayer's Multimedia Learning principles (Redundancy Effect d=0.72) and validated by field studies (84.4% of medical lecture slides violate CTML principles). The NASA Columbia disaster report provides the extreme case study of information burial via PowerPoint.
