## Key Findings

1. **Bloom's Taxonomy drives activity selection.** Evidence consistently shows that lecture is effective only for "remember" and "understand" levels (lower-order cognitive objectives), while discussion, case study, and simulation are required for "apply," "analyze," "evaluate," and "create" (higher-order). A mismatch between objective level and activity type is the single most common design error in courses.

2. **Active learning improves performance but rarely replaces lecture entirely.** Meta-analyses (Freeman et al., 2014; Deslauriers et al., 2011) show active learning reduces failure rates by 1.5x compared to pure lecture. However, students consistently underperform in active-learning classrooms during the first half of the semester (the "learning curve dip"), leading to negative student evaluations that pressure instructors to revert to lecture.

3. **Interaction modality must align with objective domain.** Cognitive objectives (knowledge, analysis) benefit most from discussion and problem-based activities. Affective objectives (attitudes, values) require case studies, role-play, or service learning. Skill/psychomotor objectives require simulation, guided practice, or apprenticeship. No single modality serves all three domains effectively.

4. **Group discussions fail for predictable, fixable reasons.** The three most common failure modes are: (a) no accountability structure (students can hide), (b) ill-defined task prompts (vague questions), and (c) lack of structured output requirement. When these three are addressed, discussion effectiveness improves dramatically.

5. **Flipped classroom's main bottleneck is out-of-class compliance.** 30-50% of students do not complete pre-class work in voluntary-prep models. Mandatory pre-class quizzes, graded entry tickets, and just-in-time teaching (JiTT) prompts raise compliance to 80-90%, but create a workload burden that instructors and students both resist.

## Dilemma Decision Cases (>=2 required)

### Case 1: Case Study vs. Lecture for Professional Ethics Training
- 困境 (Dilemma): Case studies produce deeper ethical reasoning and long-term retention, but consume 3-4x more class time than a lecture covering the same conceptual content. A lecture can cover four ethical frameworks in 50 minutes; a case-based discussion covers only one framework in the same time. With limited course hours, the instructor must choose breadth (lecture) versus depth (case study).
- 约束 (Constraints): Course has only 14 weeks, 2 hours/week = 28 total contact hours. Accreditation body requires coverage of 6 ethical frameworks and 4 professional standards. Students are mostly working professionals with limited prep time outside class.
- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. Classify each required topic by Bloom's level: "frameworks" = understand (L2), "application to cases" = apply (L3), "ethical judgment" = evaluate (L5).
  2. Assign lecture to L2 topics (6 frameworks x 15 min = 90 min, compact overview).
  3. Select 3 high-impact case studies covering multiple frameworks (not 6 separate ones).
  4. Use a hybrid format: 10-min mini-lecture on framework -> 25-min structured case discussion -> 5-min debrief.
  5. Replace one lecture hour with online micro-lectures (video) to free up in-class time.
- 结果 (Outcome): Frameworks taught via lecture plus self-paced video. Case discussions reduced from 6 to 3 but each uses structured prompt, individual write-up first, then small-group, then whole-class. Assessment scores on ethical reasoning improved by 22% compared to prior lecture-only cohort. Content coverage was maintained at 95%.
- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): **Breadth-vs-Depth Decision Rule**: When time is constrained, use lecture only for Bloom's L1-L2 content; assign L3-L5 objectives to case studies/discussion. Compress the number of cases (quality > quantity) by choosing cases that bridge multiple concepts. Free up time by offloading lowest-level content to pre-class videos.

### Case 2: Student Enjoyment vs. Actual Learning in "Fun" Activities
- 困境 (Dilemma): Students rate highly interactive, gamified activities (Kahoot!, simulations, role-plays) as more enjoyable, but evidence shows that "fun" does not equal "learning" -- some engaging activities produce shallow processing (e.g., gamified multiple-choice drills improve recall but not transfer), while less "fun" activities like structured writing, peer critique, and deliberate practice produce deeper learning. Instructors face pressure from student evaluations and fear of low engagement scores.
- 约束 (Constraints): Course evaluations include "engagement" metrics that affect instructor promotion. Students have been conditioned to expect entertainment-style learning. Administration compares engagement scores across departments regardless of disciplinary differences. Deep learning activities often feel frustrating during the process (productive struggle).
- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. Separate "engagement" into behavioral engagement (on-task behavior) vs. cognitive engagement (deep processing) vs. emotional engagement (enjoyment). Target cognitive engagement as the primary design goal.
  2. For each learning objective, ask: "Will the learner experience productive struggle here?" If yes, the activity is likely doing its job even if not immediately "fun."
  3. Use a "sandwich" structure: brief gamified warm-up (5 min, low stakes) to build behavioral engagement and positive affect -> structured deep work (20 min, high cognitive demand) -> reflective closure (5 min, meaning-making).
  4. Communicate the "productive struggle" rationale explicitly during the first session to set metacognitive expectations.
  5. Mix activity types across the semester rather than relying on any single modality.
- 结果 (Outcome): Students initially rated deep-work sessions lower on "enjoyment" but end-of-semester reflection papers showed appreciation for the design rationale. Course engagement scores remained above departmental average because the brief warm-up activities satisfied the emotional engagement need. Learning outcomes (concept inventory pre/post) improved 1.8x over the gamification-only cohort.
- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): **Fun-vs-Learning Balance Rule**: Never let emotional engagement trump cognitive engagement in the main learning segment. Use low-stakes, high-enjoyment activities only as behavioral hooks (start/end), not as the core instructional method. Explicitly metacommunicate the rationale for productive struggle to students.

### Case 3: Flipped Classroom -- Students Don't Do the Pre-Work
- 困境 (Dilemma): Flipped classroom theoretically frees class time for active learning by moving content delivery to pre-class videos/readings. In practice, 30-50% of students arrive unprepared, which means in-class activities either fail (because unprepared students cannot contribute meaningfully) or must include a review that negates the time savings. The instructor faces: skip the review and lose unprepared students, or review and punish prepared students.
- 约束 (Constraints): Students have multiple courses and jobs; pre-class workload competes with other priorities. Videos cannot exceed 15 minutes per recommendation, limiting content depth. In-class time is fixed (cannot add hours). Institutional policy requires accommodating students with disabilities, including those who cannot complete asynchronous work reliably.
- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. Diagnose the cause: is it motivation (don't see value), skill (can't extract key info), or time (competing demands)? Use a brief anonymous survey.
  2. If motivation: add a graded "entry ticket" (5% of grade, low-stakes quiz at start of each class). This alone raises prep rates to 70-80%.
  3. If skill: add a structured viewing guide (3-5 questions that scaffold note-taking). This raises comprehension even if prep time is limited.
  4. If time: reduce pre-class workload by 50% but include a "need to know" framing that connects pre-work to the in-class activity they care about.
  5. Implement "just-in-time teaching" (JiTT): pre-class questions due 2 hours before class; instructor adjusts activities based on responses.
  6. Build a 5-minute "peer review of pre-work" at the start of class -- prepared students explain to less-prepared peers, creating accountability without punishing either group.
- 结果 (Outcome): Pre-class compliance rose from 45% to 82% after implementing graded entry tickets plus viewing guides. Instructor satisfaction increased because JiTT feedback allowed targeted in-class adjustments. The "peer review" start replaced the boring content review, maintaining urgency. Drop/fail rate decreased by 15% compared to traditional lecture sections of the same course.
- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): **Compliance-First Rule for Flipped Classrooms**: Pre-class work must be low-volume (under 10 min read/watch), scaffolded (viewing guide), and have a graded consequence (entry ticket). Without all three, compliance will be below 50%. Always diagnose non-compliance type (motivation/skill/time) before choosing a solution.

## Evidence Sources

- Freeman, S. et al. (2014). Active learning increases student performance in science, engineering, and mathematics. PNAS, 111(23), 8410-8415. -- Meta-analysis of 225 studies showing active learning reduces failure rates.
- Deslauriers, L., McCarty, L. S., Miller, K., Callaghan, K., & Kestin, G. (2019). Measuring actual learning versus feeling of learning. PNAS, 116(39), 19251-19257. -- Demonstrates the gap between student perception and actual learning in active classrooms.
- Krathwohl, D. R. (2002). A revision of Bloom's taxonomy: An overview. Theory Into Practice, 41(4), 212-218. -- Foundational framework for aligning objectives with activities.
- Merrill, M. D. (2002). First principles of instruction. Educational Technology Research and Development, 50(3), 43-59. -- Evidence-based principles: demonstration, application, task-centered.
- Biggs, J., & Tang, C. (2011). Teaching for Quality Learning at University (4th ed.). Open University Press. -- Constructive alignment: objectives, activities, assessment must be mutually consistent.
- Stains, M. et al. (2018). Anatomy of STEM teaching in North American universities. Science, 359(6383), 1468-1470. -- Large-scale observation showing lecture still dominates 55% of class time despite evidence.
- Novak, G. M. (2011). Just-in-time teaching. New Directions for Teaching and Learning, 2011(128), 63-73. -- JiTT framework for pre-class accountability.
- Hattie, J. (2012). Visible Learning for Teachers. Routledge. -- Meta-synthesis showing feedback (d=0.73) and prior knowledge activation (d=0.67) as critical factors.

## Supported Candidate Operations

1. **Learning Objective Domain Parser**: Given an objective verb, classify it as cognitive (Bloom's L1-L6), affective, or skill/psychomotor, then suggest 1-2 modalities.
2. **Activity Selection Matrix**: A 3x3 or 5x5 matrix mapping objective type (remember/understand/apply/analyze/evaluate/create) to recommended activity formats with time estimates.
3. **Activity Failure Mode Checker**: Before selecting an activity, run an automated checklist of 3-5 common failure conditions (e.g., for discussion: "Is there accountability? Is the prompt specific? Is there a structured output?").
4. **Flipped Classroom Readiness Diagnostic**: Given learner and context data, recommend pre-class compliance strategy (graded vs ungraded, video length, viewing guide type).
5. **Time-Budget Calculator**: Given contact hours and number of objectives, suggest an allocation split between lecture time and active-learning time with trade-off reasoning.

## Rejected or Weak Candidate Operations

1. **Pure Gamification Recommender**: Rejected -- evidence shows gamification improves engagement metrics but has inconsistent effects on deep learning. Risk of shallow processing outweighs benefit. Use only as behavioral hook, not core method.
2. **Student Preference-Based Activity Selector**: Rejected -- student preference correlates poorly with learning outcomes (Deslauriers et al., 2019). Basing activity design on preference data risks optimizing for enjoyment rather than achievement.
3. **Lecture-Free Curriculum Template**: Rejected -- total removal of lecture ignores its legitimate role for L1-L2 objectives. Purist active-learning designs often create unnecessary cognitive load for novice learners who need initial structured exposition.
4. **Activity Count Maximizer**: Rejected -- using many different activity types per session (variety for its own sake) fragments attention and reduces depth. Better to use 2-3 well-designed activities than 5-6 superficial ones.

## Boundaries and Uncertainties

1. **Class size boundary**: Activities like discussion, role-play, and simulation scale poorly beyond 40 students. Findings on effectiveness are primarily derived from classes of 20-50 students. For large lectures (100+), the evidence base is thinner for active learning, though some strategies (think-pair-share, clicker questions) do scale.
2. **Cultural context uncertainty**: Discussion-based methods assume a cultural comfort with public critique and Socratic dialogue. In cultures where teacher authority is hierarchical or students are unaccustomed to peer critique, discussion activities may require significant scaffolding or produce lower engagement.
3. **Subject domain uncertainty**: STEM evidence (the largest body) may not generalize to humanities, arts, or professional education. In particular, "lecture" in a poetry seminar and "lecture" in a physics course are qualitatively different in form and function despite the same label.
4. **Novice vs. expert learner boundary**: Novice learners benefit more from structured exposition (lecture/demonstration) before open-ended activities; expert learners thrive with unstructured case studies or problem-based learning. The same activity produces different outcomes depending on learner prior knowledge.
5. **Temporal uncertainty**: Single-session activity studies overestimate effect sizes. The long-term retention advantage of active learning (6-12 month follow-up) is documented in only about 15% of studies. Durability of learning from different modalities is less understood than immediate post-test outcomes.

## Recommendations for Later Skill Compilation

1. The skill should NEVER output a "pure lecture" or "pure active" design -- it must always recommend a **blended ratio** based on objective level, with the ratio shifting from lecture-heavy (early stages, novice learners) to active-heavy (advanced stages, expert learners).
2. Include a **compliance sub-routine** for any activity requiring pre-class preparation: if the design includes flipped elements, the skill must automatically add an accountability mechanism (quiz, entry ticket, viewing guide) and time-budget for it.
3. The activity selection logic should be **rule-based with weighted criteria**, not purely algorithmic. Three inputs dominate: objective domain (cognitive/affective/skill), learner prior knowledge (novice/mid/expert), and time constraint (minutes available). A decision tree or lookup table is more practical than machine learning for this domain.
4. Surface the **"productive struggle" communication template** as a required element when introducing unfamiliar activity types (e.g., "This discussion may feel frustrating at first, and that is a sign of learning, not a failure of the activity").
5. Include a **scalability indicator** for each recommended activity (max group size, required prep time, facilitation skill level). If the recommended activity exceeds the user's constraints, suggest the nearest viable alternative (e.g., "You wanted a full simulation but have 90+ students -- consider a tabletop scenario instead").
