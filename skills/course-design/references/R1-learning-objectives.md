## Key Findings

1. **Verb-Level Mismatch Is the Most Common and Most Invisible Failure Point**. Bloom's Revised Taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) provides a 6-level cognitive process dimension (Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create) but most course designers stop at copying verb lists. The real operationalization gap is that a single verb (e.g., "explain") spans multiple depths -- "explain in your own words" (Understand) vs. "explain why an anomaly occurs given conflicting data" (Evaluate). Without a +Knowledge Dimension mapping (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, Metacognitive), the verb alone guarantees nothing about observable behavior.

2. **Constructive Alignment (Biggs, 1996) Breaks at Three Junctions, Not One**. The canonical 3P model (Presage-Process-Product) is widely cited but rarely audited systematically. Empirical studies (e.g., Wang et al., 2013; Hosier, 2022) show that alignment failures cluster at three distinct interfaces: (a) Intended Learning Outcomes (ILOs) to Teaching/Learning Activities (TLAs), (b) ILOs to Assessment Tasks (ATs), and (c) TLAs to ATs. The most damaging failure is (b) when ILOs target higher-order thinking (Analyze/Evaluate) but ATs test recall, because it sends the strongest signal to students about what is actually valued -- overriding any stated ILO.

3. **The "Backwash Effect" Dominates Student Behavior**. Biggs and Tang (2011) document that students orient their learning effort around assessment, not stated objectives. When assessments require only recall, students strategically disengage from higher-order learning activities regardless of what ILOs say. This means assessment-objective misalignment is not a neutral gap but an active de facto re-definition of the course's real objectives. Course designers who write "critical thinking" as an ILO but assign multiple-choice recall exams have effectively taught students that critical thinking is not actually required.

4. **Actionable Alignment Audit Checklists Exist But Are Underused in Authoring Tools**. Quality Matters (QM) General Standards 2.1-2.5 and 3.1-3.5 provide a rigorous alignment framework requiring explicit mapping between each objective, its assessment, and its learning activity. Similarly, the ASSUREmodel by Branch (2009) and the ABCD model (Audience, Behavior, Condition, Degree) for objective writing give procedural checklists. However, most course design tools (LMS authoring, curriculum mapping software) do not enforce alignment checking as a workflow gate -- they treat objectives as text metadata rather than executable constraints.

5. **Rubric-Level Calibration Is a Neglected Layer**. Even when ILOs and ATs align at the "learning outcome" level, they misalign at the rubric/assessment criteria level. For example, an ILO stating "critically evaluate competing theories" may be paired with an essay assessment (surface alignment OK), but if the rubric rewards breadth of citation rather than quality of argumentation, the actual evaluated behavior is recall, not evaluation. Course design skills must audit not just the assessment TYPE but the assessment CRITERIA against the cognitive level of the ILO.

## Dilemma Decision Cases (>=2 required)

### Case 1: Critical Thinking as Stated Objective, Recall-Based Exam as Actual Assessment

- 困境 (Dilemma): A faculty member in an introductory psychology course writes ILOs targeting "Analyze" and "Evaluate" levels (e.g., "evaluate the validity of claims in popular psychology articles"). However, departmental policy requires a common final exam consisting entirely of multiple-choice questions that test factual recall (Remember level). Redesigning the exam is blocked by departmental agreement and resource constraints (300+ students, no TA support for grading essays). The instructor faces a choice: keep the aspirational ILOs unchanged but de facto teach recall, lower the ILOs to match the exam (honest but less ambitious), or redesign assessment within constraints (e.g., add scaffolded short-answer items that approximate analysis).

- 约束 (Constraints): (1) Departmental policy mandates shared final exam, MC-only format. (2) Enrollment of 300+ students with no TA budget makes essay/scoring-rubric assessment logistically impossible. (3) The ILOs were already published in the course catalog and cannot be changed for the current term. (4) The instructor believes students "should" be capable of analysis even if it is not tested.

- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. The instructor separates "must assess" from "nice to assess" via a quick ILO triage: which ILOs represent minimum competence vs. aspirational stretch goals.
  2. She identifies that 2 of 6 ILOs are Recall-level (foundational knowledge) and can be validly assessed by MCQs. The remaining 4 target Analysis/Evaluate.
  3. She redesigns weekly low-stakes "probe" assignments: 4 per term, each a 200-word structured critique of a provided popular-science headline. These are peer-assessed in class using a 2-criterion rubric (Claim Identification + Evidence Evaluation).
  4. She revises the departmental exam to include 10 "justification" items where students select the best answer AND a distractor that requires analyzing a subtle distinction.
  5. She documents the gap for accreditation: the final exam covers ILOs 1-2 fully, ILOs 3-6 are assessed formatively via weekly probes. This makes the misalignment visible and defensible.

- 结果 (Outcome): Partial alignment achieved. The ILO-Assessment gap is not closed but is now explicitly documented. The weekly probes generate formative data that improve student performance on the analysis-adjacent exam items. Peer assessment reduces grading load to near zero. The instructor uses the gap documentation in her annual review to argue for departmental assessment reform.

- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): WHEN an assessment format constraint (e.g., department-mandated MCQ exam) is incompatible with higher-order ILOs, DO NOT change the ILOs downward. Instead, (a) decouple assessment into summative (constrained) + formative (designed for the higher level), (b) document the gap explicitly, and (c) add "wedge" items within the constrained format that approximate the higher level. This turns a binary pass/fail alignment check into a gradient: "aligned at formative level, partially aligned at summative level, gap documented."

### Case 2: "Understand" as a Catch-All Objective That Masks Multiple Cognitive Levels

- 困境 (Dilemma): A course design team for an engineering ethics module drafts all four module ILOs using the verb "understand" (e.g., "understand ethical frameworks," "understand professional responsibilities," "understand case study methodology," "understand stakeholder analysis"). The team believes "understand" is clear and safe. However, the actual learning activities include: a lecture on three ethical frameworks, a group discussion applying frameworks to a case, and a final exam requiring students to define each framework and match it to a scenario. The teaching team debates whether students "truly understood" but cannot agree on what evidence would count. The ILOs are written at a single Bloom's level (Understand), but the activities span Remember (exam definitions), Understand (lecture comprehension), Apply (case matching), and Analyze (group discussion of stakeholder trade-offs). The misalignment is invisible because "understand" masks all levels.

- 约束 (Constraints): (1) The course is part of an ABET-accredited program requiring explicit ILOs, but ABET does not prescribe cognitive levels. (2) The design team is composed of engineering faculty with no formal training in learning taxonomy; "understand" feels natural and non-controversial. (3) The module is only 3 weeks long, limiting time for redesign. (4) Different instructors teach different sections and interpret the same ILO differently.

- 决策步骤 (Decision Steps):
  1. The curriculum lead maps each ILO verb ("understand") to an observable behavior by asking: "What would a student DO to show me they have achieved this?"
  2. For "understand ethical frameworks": student can list (Remember) vs. compare (Analyze) vs. justify a choice (Evaluate). The team agrees "compare and justify" is what employers need.
  3. The lead rewrites the four ILOs with specific verbs: "Compare three ethical frameworks and justify which applies to a given professional dilemma" (Analyze/Evaluate), "Identify stakeholders in an engineering project scenario" (Understand/Apply), "Construct a stakeholder impact table for a real-world case" (Create).
  4. She realigns the final exam: replace "match framework to scenario" items with a prompt asking students to justify their framework choice for a novel scenario and explain why alternative frameworks are less appropriate.
  5. The group discussion becomes a graded in-class activity using a structured protocol (Claim-Ground-Reasoning) to give observable evidence of Analysis-level thinking.
  6. All four instructors calibrate by jointly grading 5 anonymized student responses using the new rubric, discussing discrepancies until agreement >80%.

- 结果 (Outcome): Alignment achieved. The generic "understand" ILOs are replaced with cognitively specific, observable ILOs. The assessment now targets Analyze/Evaluate rather than Remember. The calibration session surfaces three differing instructor interpretations of "compare" -- two instructors thought listing differences was sufficient, while one required evaluative judgment. The team resolves this by adding "and justify" to the rubric descriptor.

- 可提取的操作 (Extractable Operation): WHEN a course uses "understand," "know," "learn," or other non-observable verbs in ILOs, DO NOT accept the verb at face value. Instead, apply a two-step probe: (1) For each ILO, ask "What observable product or performance would convince me this student has achieved this outcome?" (2) Map the resulting product description to a Bloom's level using both the cognitive process dimension (verb) AND the knowledge dimension (what type of knowledge is being demonstrated). This forces the cognitive specificity that "understand" hides.

## Supported Candidate Operations

1. **ILO Verb Unpacking Rule**: Given any ILO using a non-observable verb ("understand," "know," "learn," "appreciate," "be aware of"), produce the most specific Bloom's-aligned observable verb pair (cognitive process + knowledge dimension) by prompting the user with the question: "What would a student DO to prove they have achieved this?" Then auto-suggest a verb from the revised taxonomy that matches the user's description.

2. **Assessment Constraint Wedge Rule**: When a course design faces a fixed assessment format that is cognitively lower than the ILO (e.g., MCQ exam vs. Evaluate-level ILO), generate a set of "wedge" assessment modifications that elevate cognitive demand within the format (e.g., justification items, best-answer-with-reasoning items, multiple-correct-answer items, two-tier MCQ items). Score each wedge on implementation cost vs. alignment gain.

3. **Three-Gate Alignment Audit**: For each ILO-Assessment-Activity triplet in a course, verify alignment at three gates:
   - Gate 1: ILO verb level matches Assessment task verb level (if not, flag as misalignment).
   - Gate 2: Assessment task verb level matches TLA verb level (if not, flag as "teaching-to-assessment gap").
   - Gate 3: Assessment criteria/rubric descriptors operationalize the same cognitive level as the ILO (if not, flag as "criteria misalignment").
   Return a traffic-light status (green/amber/red) for each gate with remediation suggestions.

## Rejected or Weak Candidate Operations

1. **Automatic Verb Taxonomy Tagger** (rejected). Simply tagging each ILO verb with its Bloom's level from a lookup table without considering the knowledge dimension or context. A verb like "compare" can be Remember (recalling a similarity the instructor mentioned), Understand (explaining a similarity in one's own words), or Evaluate (comparing to determine which is better). A naive tagger would misclassify. Replaced by the Verb Unpacking Rule which requires user context.

2. **Score-Based Alignment Metric** (weak). Computing a single numeric alignment score (e.g., "78% aligned") by averaging alignment checks across all ILOs. This hides specific failure points (one critical misalignment at Gate 2 may be worse than three minor ones at Gate 1) and encourages designers to game the metric. Prefer the traffic-light approach which preserves categorical information about where the failure occurs.

## Boundaries and Uncertainties

1. **The knowledge dimension is harder to operationalize than the cognitive process dimension.** Even expert course designers struggle to distinguish "Conceptual knowledge" from "Procedural knowledge" in a given ILO. The proposed Candidate Operations lean on the cognitive process dimension (verb level) more heavily because it is more reliably elicited from users. The operations will produce better results when paired with a visual table or example showing the four knowledge types.

2. **Constructive alignment assumes that explicit ILOs are the primary driver of course design.** In practice, many courses are designed around a textbook, a tradition, or a faculty member's research interests. The alignment operations above assume the user starts from ILOs; they are less applicable if the user starts from "what topics do we cover?" A separate transformation is needed for topic-first to objective-first conversion.

3. **No empirical threshold for "acceptable alignment."** In the literature, "complete alignment" (all ILOs matching all ATs matching all TLAs) is rare and perhaps undesirable (some formative activities should be exploratory, not aligned to a specific ILO). The operations should include an "intentional misalignment" flag for cases where the designer chooses to deviate from alignment (e.g., a generative discussion that is not assessed) rather than being forced by constraint.

4. **Single-course alignment does not address program-level or credential-level coherence.** An individual course ILO may be intentionally set at the Remember level because it is the first course in a sequence, with higher-level ILOs built in later courses. The alignment operations currently scope to a single course/module and would falsely flag this as too low. A "program-level context" parameter should be added to suppress warnings when an ILO is intentionally foundational.

## Recommendations for Later Skill Compilation

1. **Embed the Three-Gate Alignment Audit as a sub-skill** that is triggered whenever a user asks to "review," "check," or "audit" a course design. This gives immediate practical value and scaffolds the user toward thinking in terms of ILO-AT-TLA triplets.

2. **Create a "Verb Refinement Wizard"** as a guided interaction: present the user with their raw ILO, ask the observable-evidence question, suggest a specific Bloom's verb + knowledge-dimension pair, then propagate the new verb to the assessment and activity design. This avoids overwhelming the user with taxonomy theory while achieving the alignment benefit.

3. **Include a "Constraint Input" step** early in the skill flow: before auditing alignment, ask the user to list fixed constraints (departmental exam policy, class size, TA support, accreditation requirements). This prevents the skill from generating alignment recommendations that are immediately rejected as infeasible and primes the user for the Wedge Rule approach.

4. **Provide a "Partial Alignment Acceptable" template** for documenting alignment gaps in accreditation reviews. Many course designers know gaps exist but cannot articulate them in defensible language. A structured template (Stated ILO | Actual Assessment | Gap Description | Rationale | Remediation Plan) turns misalignment from a weakness into evidence of reflective practice.

5. **Flag "understand" as a systemic anti-pattern** with a dedicated check: scan all ILOs for the words "understand," "know," "learn," "appreciate," "be aware of," and "be familiar with." When found, do NOT auto-replace -- instead, initiate the Verb Unpacking Rule dialog. This single intervention catches the majority of alignment problems at their source.
